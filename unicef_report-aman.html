Introduction

Welcome to a data-driven exploration of children’s welfare across the globe. This report investigates two critical UNICEF indicators:

  • Indicator 1: Percentage of children suffering five or more simultaneous deprivations
  • Indicator 2: Uptake of zinc treatment for diarrhoea among children under 5

Diarrhoea kills hundreds of thousands of under-5s each year, yet zinc supplementation — simple and cheap — cuts its duration and severity significantly. Multidimensional child poverty remains concentrated in regions least equipped to address it. Together, these indicators reveal where children are most vulnerable and where progress is stalling.

1. Global Child Deprivation: A World Map

Children suffering from five simultaneous deprivations represent the most severely disadvantaged group. The map shows each country’s most recently recorded deprivation rate. Darker shades indicate higher proportions of children in acute deprivation.

# ── 1. World Map – Child Deprivation ─────────────────────────────────────────

dep_latest = (
    ind1
    .filter(pl.col('sex') == 'Total')
    .sort('time_period', descending=True)
    .unique(subset=['country'], keep='first')
    .select(['alpha_3_code', 'obs_value'])
    .rename({'obs_value': 'deprivation_pct'})
)

# Load world geometry from Natural Earth via URL (geopandas.datasets deprecated)
NE_URL = (
    'https://raw.githubusercontent.com/nvkelso/natural-earth-vector/'
    'master/geojson/ne_110m_admin_0_countries.geojson'
)
world = gpd.read_file(NE_URL)
# ADM0_A3 is the most complete ISO-A3 equivalent in Natural Earth data
world = world.rename(columns={'ADM0_A3': 'alpha_3_code'})
dep_pd = dep_latest.to_pandas()
world_merged = world.merge(dep_pd, on='alpha_3_code', how='left')

fig, ax = plt.subplots(1, 1, figsize=(14, 7))

world_merged[world_merged['deprivation_pct'].isna()].plot(
    ax=ax, color='#e8e8e8', edgecolor='#cccccc', linewidth=0.3
)
world_merged.dropna(subset=['deprivation_pct']).plot(
    column='deprivation_pct',
    cmap='YlOrRd',
    linewidth=0.3,
    edgecolor='#cccccc',
    ax=ax,
    legend=True,
    legend_kwds={
        'label': 'Children with 5+ Deprivations (%)',
        'orientation': 'horizontal',
        'shrink': 0.5,
        'pad': 0.02
    }
)

ax.set_title(
    'Children Suffering Five or More Deprivations (Latest Year)',
    fontsize=14, fontweight='bold', pad=15
)
ax.set_axis_off()
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

Key insight: Sub-Saharan African and South Asian countries register the highest deprivation rates — often exceeding 60–80% — while Europe and the Americas report negligible levels. Grey countries (no data) represent a surveillance and reporting gap, often in the most fragile states.

2. Zinc Treatment Uptake by Gender

Zinc treatment is a low-cost, high-impact intervention for child diarrhoea. The chart examines the top 15 countries by treatment uptake, split by sex to reveal any gender-based access gaps.

# ── 2. Bar Chart – Zinc Treatment by Country & Sex ───────────────────────────

zinc_gender = (
    ind2
    .filter(pl.col('sex').is_in(['Male', 'Female']))
    .sort('time_period', descending=True)
    .unique(subset=['country', 'sex'], keep='first')
)

top15_countries = (
    zinc_gender
    .group_by('country')
    .agg(pl.mean('obs_value').alias('avg'))
    .sort('avg', descending=True)
    .head(15)
    .select('country')
)

zinc_top15 = zinc_gender.join(top15_countries, on='country', how='inner')

(
    ggplot(zinc_top15.to_pandas(), aes(x='reorder(country, obs_value)', y='obs_value', fill='sex'))
    + geom_col(position=position_dodge(width=0.8), width=0.7, alpha=0.9)
    + scale_fill_manual(values={'Male': '#2196F3', 'Female': '#E91E63'})
    + coord_flip()
    + labs(
        title='Top 15 Countries: Zinc Treatment Uptake for Child Diarrhoea',
        subtitle='Split by sex — most recent available year per country',
        x=None,
        y='% of Under-5 Children Receiving Zinc',
        fill='Sex'
    )
    + theme_minimal()
    + theme(
        figure_size=(11, 7),
        plot_title=element_text(size=13, weight='bold'),
        plot_subtitle=element_text(size=10, color='#555555'),
        legend_position='bottom',
        panel_grid_major_y=element_blank(),
        panel_grid_minor=element_blank(),
        axis_text=element_text(size=9)
    )
)

Key insight: In top-performing countries, zinc uptake frequently exceeds 40–50%, but gender differences are generally small — access barriers are country-level rather than sex-linked. Inter-country variation is stark, pointing to disparate health system capacity.

3. Trend Over Time: Zinc Treatment (Selected Countries)

The time series tracks zinc treatment uptake across countries with multiple data points, revealing whether coverage is improving, stagnating, or regressing.

# ── 3. Time Series – Zinc Treatment Trend ────────────────────────────────────

zinc_trend = (
    ind2
    .filter(pl.col('sex') == 'Total')
    .group_by('country')
    .agg(pl.count('time_period').alias('n_obs'))
    .filter(pl.col('n_obs') >= 3)
    .join(
        ind2.filter(pl.col('sex') == 'Total').select(['country', 'time_period', 'obs_value']),
        on='country',
        how='inner'
    )
    .sort('time_period')
)

top8 = (
    zinc_trend
    .group_by('country')
    .agg(pl.mean('obs_value').alias('avg'))
    .sort('avg', descending=True)
    .head(8)
    .select('country')
)

zinc_trend_top8 = zinc_trend.join(top8, on='country', how='inner')

palette = [
    '#E63946', '#457B9D', '#2A9D8F', '#E9C46A',
    '#F4A261', '#264653', '#A8DADC', '#6D6875'

(
    ggplot(zinc_trend_top8.to_pandas(), aes(x='time_period', y='obs_value', color='country'))
    + geom_line(size=1.2, alpha=0.9)
    + geom_point(size=2.5)
    + scale_color_manual(values=palette)
    + scale_x_continuous(breaks=list(range(2005, 2025, 3)))
    + labs(
        title='Zinc Treatment Coverage Trends (2005–2024)',
        subtitle='Countries with 3+ observations — Total (both sexes)',
        x='Year',
        y='% Children Under-5 Receiving Zinc',
        color='Country'
    )
    + theme_minimal()
    + theme(
        figure_size=(12, 6),
        plot_title=element_text(size=13, weight='bold'),
        plot_subtitle=element_text(size=10, color='#555555'),
        legend_position='right',
        legend_title=element_text(size=9, weight='bold'),
        panel_grid_minor=element_blank(),
        axis_text=element_text(size=9)
    )
)

Key insight: Coverage trajectories vary considerably. Some countries show steady upward trends, suggesting effective scale-up programmes; others plateau or decline — likely reflecting funding gaps, supply chain issues, or post-COVID health priority shifts.

4. GDP Per Capita vs. Child Deprivation

Does national wealth correlate with child welfare outcomes? Each country is positioned by GDP per capita against its child deprivation rate, with population as bubble size.

# ── 4. Scatter – GDP per Capita vs Child Deprivation ─────────────────────────

dep_for_scatter = (
    ind1
    .filter(pl.col('sex') == 'Total')
    .sort('time_period', descending=True)
    .unique(subset=['country'], keep='first')
    .select(['alpha_3_code', 'country', 'obs_value', 'time_period'])
    .rename({'obs_value': 'deprivation_pct'})
)

meta_latest = (
    meta
    .sort('year', descending=True)
    .unique(subset=['alpha_3_code'], keep='first')
    .select([
        'alpha_3_code',
        pl.col('GDP per capita (constant 2015 US$)').alias('gdp_pc'),
        pl.col('Population, total').alias('population'),
        pl.col('Life expectancy at birth, total (years)').alias('life_exp')
    ])
)

scatter_df = (
    dep_for_scatter
    .join(meta_latest, on='alpha_3_code', how='inner')
    .drop_nulls(subset=['gdp_pc', 'deprivation_pct', 'population'])
    .with_columns([
        (pl.col('population') / 1_000_000).alias('pop_m'),
        pl.col('gdp_pc').log(base=10).alias('log_gdp')
    ])
)

(
    ggplot(scatter_df.to_pandas(), aes(x='log_gdp', y='deprivation_pct', size='pop_m', color='deprivation_pct'))
    + geom_point(alpha=0.7)
    + geom_smooth(method='lm', color='#333333', fill='#cccccc', alpha=0.2, size=0.8)
    + scale_color_gradient(low='#ffd700', high='#cc0000')
    + guides(color='none')
    + scale_y_continuous(limits=(0, 100))
    + labs(
        title='GDP Per Capita vs. Child Deprivation Rate',
        subtitle='Bubble size = population (millions) · X-axis on log₁₀ scale',
        x='GDP Per Capita, log₁₀ (constant 2015 US$)',
        y='Children with 5+ Deprivations (%)',
        size='Population (M)'
    )
    + theme_minimal()
    + theme(
        figure_size=(11, 7),
        plot_title=element_text(size=13, weight='bold'),
        plot_subtitle=element_text(size=10, color='#555555'),
        panel_grid_minor=element_blank(),
        legend_position='right',
        axis_text=element_text(size=9)
    )
)

Key insight: There is a clear negative relationship — wealthier countries have dramatically fewer children in severe deprivation. The steepest drop occurs in the low-to-middle income bracket, suggesting early economic development yields the largest welfare dividends for children.

5. Life Expectancy vs. Zinc Treatment Uptake

This chart cross-references life expectancy at birth against zinc treatment coverage to test whether broader healthcare quality predicts better diarrhoea management specifically.

# ── 5. Scatter – Life Expectancy vs Zinc Uptake ──────────────────────────────

zinc_latest = (
    ind2
    .filter(pl.col('sex') == 'Total')
    .sort('time_period', descending=True)
    .unique(subset=['alpha_3_code'], keep='first')
    .select(['alpha_3_code', 'obs_value'])
    .rename({'obs_value': 'zinc_pct'})
)

meta_life = (
    meta
    .sort('year', descending=True)
    .unique(subset=['alpha_3_code'], keep='first')
    .select([
        'alpha_3_code',
        pl.col('Life expectancy at birth, total (years)').alias('life_exp'),
        pl.col('GDP per capita (constant 2015 US$)').alias('gdp_pc')
    ])
)

le_zinc_df = (
    zinc_latest
    .join(meta_life, on='alpha_3_code', how='inner')
    .drop_nulls()
)

(
    ggplot(le_zinc_df.to_pandas(), aes(x='life_exp', y='zinc_pct', color='gdp_pc'))
    + geom_point(size=3, alpha=0.75)
    + geom_smooth(method='loess', color='#333333', fill='#dddddd', alpha=0.25, size=0.8)
    + scale_color_gradient(low='#d73027', high='#1a9850', name='GDP/capita\n(2015 US$)')
    + labs(
        title='Life Expectancy vs. Zinc Treatment Coverage for Child Diarrhoea',
        subtitle='Each point is a country. Colour indicates GDP per capita.',
        x='Life Expectancy at Birth (years)',
        y='Zinc Uptake Among Under-5s with Diarrhoea (%)'
    )
    + theme_minimal()
    + theme(
        figure_size=(11, 6),
        plot_title=element_text(size=13, weight='bold'),
        plot_subtitle=element_text(size=10, color='#555555'),
        panel_grid_minor=element_blank(),
        legend_position='right',
        axis_text=element_text(size=9)
    )
)

Key insight: Zinc uptake is not simply highest where life expectancy is highest. Middle-income countries — where diarrhoea burden persists but some delivery infrastructure exists — show the greatest uptake. This implies zinc programmes are correctly targeted: where the need exists and delivery capacity does too.

Conclusion

This report examined two complementary lenses on child health: the breadth of deprivation and the depth of a specific intervention (zinc for diarrhoea). Together they tell a consistent story:

  • Geography is destiny for child welfare. Sub-Saharan Africa and parts of South Asia face deprivation rates that reflect systemic failures — poverty, weak health systems, and limited infrastructure all compound each other.
  • Wealth matters, but not linearly. The sharpest welfare gains occur in the transition from low to middle income, not from middle to high.
  • Targeted interventions can work independently of overall health system quality. Zinc coverage is often highest where need meets some delivery capacity — but that window is narrow.
  • Data gaps are a problem in themselves. Grey countries on the map tend to be the most fragile states, where the need is likely highest.

The path to improvement demands country-specific strategies, sustained funding for low-cost interventions like zinc, and investment in the data infrastructure needed to monitor progress honestly.
